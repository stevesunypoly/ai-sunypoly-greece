AI Literacy: Learner's Permit

A Content-Neutral 15-Exercise Scaffold

Before You Start

You have a Learner's Permit.

With a learner's permit, you can drive. You can go places. You can even go alone. What you cannot do — yet — is submit AI-generated work in this course. That privilege requires a license. The 15 exercises are how you earn it.

[TOPIC] is a placeholder. Your instructor will replace it with a specific subject. The exercises work for any discipline.

Prerequisites: Your Toolkit

Before Exercise 1, you need three things working.

1. A model. Access to at least one large language model — ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, or equivalent. You will use more than one before this course is over.

2. A transcript exporter. Every model handles conversation export differently. What matters is that you can save the full text of any conversation to a file. - ChatGPT: Use the ChatGPT Exporter browser extension (Chrome), or share/export from the menu. - Claude: Download conversation from the menu. - Gemini and others: Check current export options — these change. If there is no export function, copy the full conversation into a document.

The skill is not the button. The skill is: you own your conversations. Save them. The model won't.

3. A repository. A cloud folder with managed access and shareable links — SharePoint, OneDrive, Google Drive, or institutional equivalent. Create a folder called AI Literacy now. Inside it, create two subfolders: - /Transcripts — every exported conversation goes here - /Artifacts — every model-generated output you produce goes here

This folder is your AI Workbench. It belongs to you, not to any model. It works regardless of which AI you use. You will build it across all 15 exercises.

Three Practices That Govern Every Exercise

RTW is the spine of every exercise: - Read — engage carefully with what the model generates - Think — evaluate it; agree, disagree, push back, extend - Write — this means prompting. When you write a prompt, you are writing. The model's response is the machine's writing. Together, you are engaged in collaborative writing: your prompts shape what the model generates, and what the model generates shapes your next prompt. The goal is to write prompts that cause the model to produce text that serves your purposes.

The model gets the last word. You think, you prompt, you direct — but the artifact is always the model's final response. You shape it. The model writes it. The transcript holds the whole story.

Artifact + Transcript. Starting in Exercise 2, every exercise produces two things. The artifact is the model's last response, copied into a document and saved to /Artifacts. The transcript is the full exported conversation, saved to /Transcripts. Neither is optional. Neither is supplementary. Both are the work.

Phase I — Learning to Drive (Exercises 1–5)

You and the model. Learn the instrument before you play it.

Exercise 1 — First Conversation

Have a real conversation with the model about [TOPIC]. At least 3 prompts. Follow your curiosity. Push back on something. Ask a follow-up.

Read what the model gives you. Notice where it's confident. Notice where you're skeptical.

Think: What did you expect? What surprised you?

That's it. There is no artifact yet.

Transcript: Export the full conversation. Save it to /Transcripts. Title it Ex1-[yourname].

This is your first record. Keep every record from here forward. The model will not remember this conversation. You will. The workbench is where memory lives.

Exercise 2 — Compression and Progression

Prompt:

"Summarize [TOPIC] in 25 words."

Read it. This is your baseline.

Now expand — but not into a longer summary. A deeper version:

Prompt:

"Now expand that to [your chosen number] words — not a new summary, a deeper version of the same one. Someone who read the 25-word version should feel like they're going further, not starting over."

Choose your own multiplier. Choose your own number of levels. Common patterns: 25→100→400, or 25→75→225, or 33→100→300. Pick something and try it.

The goal: by the time you reach the longest version, you should feel like you already knew most of what you just read. Each level confirms and extends — it doesn't surprise from scratch.

Think, genuinely: At which level did [TOPIC] start to feel familiar? When did you begin recognizing ideas rather than encountering them for the first time? Is this a useful way for you to learn — or does it flatten things you'd rather encounter at full depth? Tell the model your honest answer.

Now ask the model for something useful — not a summary of the summaries, but something personal:

Prompt:

"Based on our conversation, generate something I could actually keep — a personal reference, a key question I should hold onto, a way of remembering this, or something else suited to how I said I learn. Make it for me specifically."

Artifact: That response. Copy it into a document. Save to /Artifacts. Title it Ex2-[yourname].

Transcript: Export and save to /Transcripts. Title it Ex2-[yourname].

You now have a workbench with your first artifact and your first two transcripts.

Exercise 3 — Push Back

Return to your Exercise 1 transcript — the model's unguided, unedited output before you shaped anything. Find a claim that you want to push on.

You have four moves. Use whichever fits:

  • "I don't understand this." Ask it to explain differently. Don't accept the first re-explanation.
  • "My experience tells me otherwise." Tell it what you've observed or learned that pulls against its claim.
  • "This reading says something different." Paste a contradicting sentence from any source — an article, a textbook, anything.
  • "Here's another model disagreeing with you." Take the same prompt to a different AI, paste that response back, and ask this model to respond.

Each of these is a different cognitive move. Use at least one. Explore more if you want.

Think: Did the model defend its position — or did it fold because you pushed? Models are trained to be agreeable. Agreeable is not the same as accurate. Notice the difference.

Prompt:

"Summarize this exchange in 3–4 sentences. Capture what I challenged, how you responded, and what the disagreement — or clarification — revealed about [TOPIC]."

Artifact: That response. Save to /Artifacts. Title it Ex3-[yourname].

Transcript: Export and save to /Transcripts. Title it Ex3-[yourname].

This is the first exercise with an explicit closing prompt — you ask the model to summarize the exchange as its final response, and that response becomes your artifact. In Phases I and II, your artifacts will mostly be reflections like this one. In Phase III, they become documents.

Exercise 4 — Trust Audit

Prompt:

"What are the limits of your knowledge about [TOPIC]? Where should I not trust you?"

Read carefully. Models are sometimes honestly uncertain here — and sometimes evasive in ways that sound like honesty. The difference matters.

Think: Does its list match your skepticism from Exercises 1–3? Are there gaps it didn't flag that you'd expect it to acknowledge?

Respond in the chat: 3–5 sentences on what you now trust and don't trust this model to do with [TOPIC].

Prompt:

"Incorporate my response into a short artifact titled 'Trust Audit: [TOPIC].' Capture my assessment and add anything you think I should also be skeptical about."

Artifact: That Trust Audit. Save to /Artifacts. Title it Ex4-[yourname].

Transcript: Export and save to /Transcripts. Title it Ex4-[yourname].

Exercise 5 — Deep Conversation (15 Minutes)

Set a timer for 15 minutes. Have a genuine, sustained conversation with the model about [TOPIC] — any aspect that interests you. Follow threads. Disagree. Ask it to explain things differently. Go somewhere you didn't plan to go.

Don't clean it up. Don't start over if it goes sideways. The mess is part of the record.

When the timer ends, stop. Write your reflections directly in the chat as your next message: - What surprised you? - Where was the model most useful? Least? - What question did you not get to? - What would you do differently next time?

Your reflections are now part of the transcript.

Then choose one of the following closing prompts — whichever format is most useful to you:

Option A: "Write a 150-word summary of what I engaged with in this conversation, organized as 3–4 bullet points — like a slide I could review later."

Option B: "Give me the 3 most important questions this conversation raised that I haven't answered yet."

Option C: "Write a short paragraph — 100 words — capturing the central insight of this conversation and what I should explore next."

Option D: "Give me a metaphor or analogy that captures the most important idea we discussed about [TOPIC]."

Artifact: That response. Save to /Artifacts. Title it Ex5-[yourname].

Transcript: Full, unedited conversation including your written reflections. Export and save to /Transcripts. Title it Ex5-[yourname].

Phase II — Building Skill (Exercises 6–10)

You can operate the vehicle. Now drive it where you want to go.

Exercise 6 — Fork: New Model, Full Context

Open a completely new conversation — in a different model than you used in Exercise 5.

Your first message is not a question. It is context. Upload your Exercise 5 transcript file to the new conversation.

Prompt:

"I'm uploading a conversation I had with another AI about [TOPIC]. Please read it carefully. I'll ask you to continue the work after."

Wait for it to confirm it has read the context. Then ask the question you didn't get to in Exercise 5 — the one you noted in your reflection.

Read the response. Compare the behavior, not just the content. Does this model handle context differently? Does it respond to the same ideas with different tone, assumptions, or depth?

Ask at least 3 follow-up questions.

Write your comparison in the chat before the closing prompt: one short paragraph on what felt different about this model.

Prompt:

"Summarize what this conversation added to my understanding of [TOPIC] that the previous conversation did not. Be specific about what was new, different, or contradictory."

Artifact: That comparison summary. Save to /Artifacts. Title it Ex6-[yourname].

Transcript: Export and save to /Transcripts. Title it Ex6-[yourname].

You carried your own context from one model to another. The model did not do that. You did. You are the memory. The workbench is where memory lives.

Exercise 7 — Same Content, Three Audiences

Prompt:

"Explain [TOPIC] three times: once for a curious 10-year-old, once for a first-year college student, once for a working professional in the field."

Read all three. Note where the facts change versus where only the framing changes. Note what the professional version assumes you already know.

Think: Which version is most honest about complexity? Which is most useful? Are those the same answer?

Use one of your four moves from Exercise 3 to push on one of the versions.

Prompt:

"Write a short artifact analyzing the structural differences between these three versions — tone, assumptions, density. Not the content: the architecture. Then note which version served me best as a learner and why."

Artifact: That analysis. Save to /Artifacts. Title it Ex7-[yourname].

Transcript: Export and save. Title it Ex7-[yourname].

Exercise 8 — First Reading

Your instructor will provide a reading related to [TOPIC]. Upload the PDF.

Prompt:

"I'm uploading a reading about [TOPIC]. For this exercise, base your responses only on this document — not on your general knowledge. Tell me when you are drawing from the text versus inferring beyond it."

Then work through the reading in stages:

Stage 1 — Overview:

"Summarize the main argument of this reading in 50 words."

Stage 2 — Dissect:

"Identify the two most important paragraphs. Explain why they are central to the argument. Quote each one directly."

Stage 3 — Challenge: Use one of your four moves from Exercise 3. Push on something in the text — something you don't understand, something that contradicts your experience, or something another source handles differently.

Stage 4 — Extract:

"Generate a citable reference for this reading in APA format, including DOI if available."

Think across all four stages: What does the model do well when confined to a single source? What does it struggle with? Where does it seem to slip outside the text despite your instructions?

Prompt:

"Summarize what this reading is about, what I engaged with, and what I'm still uncertain about — based only on our exchange. Do not draw on outside knowledge."

Artifact: That summary. Save to /Artifacts. Title it Ex8-[yourname].

Transcript: Export and save. This transcript should show the disagreement — don't tidy it up. Title it Ex8-[yourname].

Exercise 9 — Second Reading and Comparison

Your instructor will provide a second reading related to [TOPIC]. Upload it.

Prompt:

"I'm uploading a second reading about [TOPIC]. Again, base your responses only on this document for now."

Repeat the four-stage process from Exercise 8: 1. 50-word summary 2. Two key paragraphs with direct quotes 3. A challenge using one of your four moves 4. APA citation with DOI

Then open the comparison:

Prompt:

"Now you may draw on both readings. Where do they agree? Where do they contradict each other? What does each one say that the other doesn't?"

Think: The model just held two sources in tension. How well did it do? Did it flatten the differences or represent them honestly?

Prompt:

"Write an artifact comparing these two readings — their main arguments, their points of agreement and disagreement, and what together they suggest about [TOPIC]. This should be something I could use as a study reference."

Artifact: That comparison. Save to /Artifacts. Title it Ex9-[yourname].

Transcript: Export and save. Title it Ex9-[yourname].

Exercise 10 — Build Your Workbench

You now have 9 transcripts and 8 artifacts. This exercise is about stepping back and thinking about what you've built — and what to do with it.

Open a new conversation. Upload your artifacts from Exercises 2–9.

Prompt:

"I'm uploading a set of AI-generated artifacts from my own conversations about [TOPIC]. What do they suggest about what I understand well, what remains unclear, and what questions I keep returning to?"

Read its analysis. Does it see patterns you didn't?

Then ask it to think about your workbench as a system:

Prompt:

"I have transcripts and artifacts from multiple AI conversations spread across a repository folder. I want to be able to use this material in future conversations without re-reading everything each time. What would a good synthesis document look like? What should it contain? What are the risks of relying on one instead of the originals? Why might generating one still be a good idea?"

Read this answer carefully. The model is reflecting on AI-assisted knowledge management — including the risks. That reflection is worth taking seriously.

Prompt:

"Summarize your recommendations for organizing my workbench and your honest assessment of the tradeoffs of synthesis over source material."

Artifact: That summary. Save to /Artifacts. Title it Ex10-[yourname].

Transcript: Export and save. Title it Ex10-[yourname].

Your workbench is now a subject of study, not just a storage system.

Phase III — Independent Operation (Exercises 11–15)

Minimal supervision. You set the destination, manage the route, document the trip.

From here, your artifacts are not reflections on conversations. They are documents — the kind you would turn in as coursework or share as outputs. Every artifact from Exercise 11 forward carries full citation in this format:

Generated with AI assistance. Student: [your name] Date: [today's date] Model: [model name] Transcript: [your repository link]

Exercise 11 — Teach Yourself

Prompt:

"Create a learning sequence that teaches me [TOPIC] at four levels of depth. Use a consistent multiplier — 25, 100, 400, 1600 words works well, but choose your own. Each level must build on the last: by the time I reach the longest version, I should feel like I already know most of it."

Work through each level actively. After each, tell the model what was new versus what you already understood from the previous level. Push back if a level adds filler instead of depth.

Prompt:

"Compile the full sequence as a single clean document. Add full citation at the end."

Artifact: That compiled sequence. First artifact with full citation. Save to /Artifacts. Title it Ex11-[yourname].

Transcript: Export and save. Title it Ex11-[yourname].

Exercise 12 — Synthesis Document

This is the most complex exercise in the course. You are going to use the model to synthesize your entire workbench — and then ask it to explain what it did and why.

Upload your artifacts from Exercises 4–11, plus any readings from Exercises 8–9.

Prompt:

"I'm uploading my artifacts, transcripts, and readings from a course on [TOPIC]. Read them carefully. Then generate a synthesis document: a single, organized overview of what I've learned, what questions remain open, what the key ideas are, and what I should carry into my next conversation or project. This document should work as the opening context for a conversation with any model."

Read the synthesis. Push back on anything it misses or distorts.

Then ask it to reflect on what it just did:

Prompt:

"Explain what you did to generate this synthesis. What did you include and exclude, and why? What are the risks of relying on a document like this instead of the original sources? Why might it still be worth doing?"

Read this carefully. The model is describing its own process of compression and selection — and naming the tradeoffs. This is worth understanding before you depend on the output.

Prompt:

"Produce a final synthesis document incorporating any corrections I've made, followed by your reflection on its limits. Add full citation at the end."

Artifact: The synthesis document with reflection. Save to /Artifacts. Title it Ex12-Synthesis-[yourname]. This is the most important artifact in your workbench — it is portable, model-agnostic, and designed to seed any future conversation.

Transcript: Export and save. Title it Ex12-[yourname].

Exercise 13 — Did You Learn Your Own Slop?

Upload your synthesis document and artifacts from Exercises 6–12.

Prompt:

"Based only on these artifacts — generated from my own conversations about [TOPIC] — create a 5-question quiz that tests whether I actually understand the material. Weight toward application and judgment, not recall. Draw questions from what I wrote, not from general knowledge of [TOPIC]."

Take the quiz in the chat. Give your actual answers.

Prompt:

"Evaluate my answers honestly. Where did I demonstrate real understanding? Where did I reproduce language from my own artifacts without understanding it?"

Think: The quiz is drawn from your own outputs. Did you learn what you generated — or did you generate without learning? Both are valid data.

Prompt:

"Write a document summarizing the quiz, my answers, your evaluation, and what this reveals about the relationship between generating and understanding. Add full citation."

Artifact: That document. Save to /Artifacts. Title it Ex13-[yourname]. Include your actual answers — don't clean up wrong ones.

Transcript: Export and save. Title it Ex13-[yourname].

Exercise 14 — Go Public

Prompt:

"Write a neutral, structured, Wikipedia-style article on [TOPIC]. Section headings, neutral tone, no first person, no hedging. Suitable for a general reader with no background."

Read it as a reader, not as its author. Would you trust this if you found it? What would you verify?

Use your disagreement moves. Prompt any corrections. When it's right:

Prompt:

"Final version, formatted cleanly. Add full citation at the end."

Artifact: The article. Save to /Artifacts. Title it Ex14-[yourname].

Transcript: Export and save, including all corrections. Title it Ex14-[yourname].

Exercise 15 — Road Test

No scaffolding. You drive.

The task: Produce a substantive artifact about [TOPIC] in a format of your choosing — briefing, explainer, analysis, lesson plan, annotated summary, structured argument. Use your synthesis document as opening context. Use whatever model you choose. Use whatever prompting strategies you have built.

The artifact must: 1. Be the model's final response, shaped entirely by your prompting 2. Carry full citation 3. End with a Prompter's Note — 3 sentences: what you tried, what didn't work, what you'd do differently

The transcript must: 1. Be full and unedited 2. Show the iteration — including what failed 3. Be saved to /Transcripts and linked inside the artifact

Artifact: Save to /Artifacts. Title it Ex15-[yourname]. Citation and Prompter's Note inside.

Transcript: Save to /Transcripts. Title it Ex15-[yourname].

When both are accepted: you are licensed.

After Licensing

You may submit AI-generated work in this course.

The standing rule: Every AI-assisted artifact must carry full citation — your name, date, model, and a live link to its transcript in your repository.

Your workbench stays with you. It is portable, model-agnostic, and yours. Fifteen transcripts. Fifteen artifacts. A synthesis document. A quiz you took on your own outputs. These are not just course deliverables — they are the beginning of a practice.

The insight is yours. The transcript proves it.

Future Implementation Note

This scaffold is designed to run in any environment where students can access a model and a repository. A Brightspace or equivalent LMS container could house the artifact submissions, link to repository folders, and track licensing completion across the course. That integration is a logical next step — not a prerequisite. The workbench works without it.
